Ledger Accounts

  • Boniface Kingori
  • Nov 10, 2022

For each and every item in the business a ledger account is maintained. This is a “T” format account that records for increase or decrease of that specific item. At the end of a financial period, the details in this ledger accounts are summarized to derive the balance total of that item that is used in the preparation of final statement as well as opening balance for the next period. Broadly all the items in a business are classified under the following categories:

i. ../assets
ii. Capital
iii. Liabilities
iv. Expenses
v. Incomes

The recording into this ledger accounts follows the double entry system. The double entry system is based on the ovality concept ************ which is an accounting guideline that states that every transaction in accounting should have a double effect i.e. it should be recorded twice.
This double entry system therefore guides on the recording of ../assets, capital, liabilities and income into their respective and specific ledger accounts.

The format of this ledger account looks as shown below:

The left side of the account is referred to as the DEBIT SIDE and the right side of the account is referred to as the CREDIT SIDE.
The date column shows the opening period for the account and the specific date for various transactions.
Details column shows the account affected by that transaction more so the other corresponding account.
Amount colum shows the amount involved in the transaction.

1. Recording for ../assets
../assets are a business resources the enable it to carry out its trading activities. They are classified into two:

i. Non-current ../assets.
ii. Current ../assets.

Non-current ../assets: ../assets expected to last in the business for a period larger than one year. Such resources include, land, buildings, plant and machinery, furniture, equipment, motor vehicles etc.
Current ../assets: ../assets not expected to be in the business for more than one year. They include: stock, debtors, cash in hand, cash at bank, pre-payments. When recording for ../assets each specific asset should have its own ledger account. The recording for ../assets is that an increase of ../assets is debited. A decrease in ../assets are credited.

Example

On 22nd June jane bought a motor van by cheque sh250000.
Account affected.

motor van account – DR (increase)
bank account – CR (decrease)

2. Accounting for liabilities
Liabilities refer to debts that our business owes to other parties as a result of past trading activities. These are the obligation of the business to other parties.
Liabilities are categorized into 2:
non-current liabilities and current liabilities

i. Non- current liabilities. These are obligation that are expected to last or being paid after one year, i.e. they take a period longer that 1 year to mature e.g. long-term loans.
ii. Current liabilities Obligations that last for a period of less than 1 year and therefore will be paid within 1 year.
Example include: creditors, banks, short-term loans.
The double entry for liabilities is:

a. Increase in liabilities are credited.
b. Decrease in liabilities are debited.

Example

On 29th Jane bought office furniture sh.4000 on credit from office master limited.
on 30th jane paid office masters sh.2000 cash.
Transactions.29th Account affected.

Office furniture (increase) DR
Office masters limited (decrease) CR.

Transaction 30th Account affected

office masters limited (decrease) DR.
Cash account (increase) CR

3. Account for capital
Capital: this is the funding that the owners put in the business to finance the ../assets. The accounting treatment for capital is that:

a. Increase in capital are credited.
b. decrease in capital are debited.

Are mainly due to thing like drawings, they are therefore capture in a separate account (debited) or other specific terms that capital.

Example

On 1st June 2022 started a business with ksh.50000 in the bank Account affected: -

capital account increase (Cr).
Bank account decrease (Dr).

4. Accounting for expenses.
Expenses are amount paid out for services rendered to our business. Expenses have a recurrent nature to mean that we incur them repeatedly with the intention of contributing to revenue generation.
Side note: A business must incur costs (expenditure) that ensures it to carry out its trading activities. This expenditures are broadly classified as revenue expenditure.
Revenue expenditures: expenditures on e.g. stock and expenses that help in generating income.
Capital expenditures: expenditure on capital items (non- current ../assets) that enhance long-term growth of the business.
Examples of expenses include: salaries, telephone bills, motor vehicles running, bank charges, rent expenses, electricity, stationery, office expenses, insurance, advertising etc.
The accounting treatment for expenses in that:

a. Increase in expense are debited (Dr).
b. Decrease would be Dr. (expenses are rarely credited, once a service is paid for cannot be returned.

For each individual expenses a specific ledger account is maintained.

Example

On 30th June paid monthly salary wages, paid cash ksh.40000
Affected accounts:

wages Account: increase (Dr).
Cash account: decrease (Cr).

5. Accounting for incomes.
Incomes: revenues that a business generated from its trading activities as well as any other income generating activities. Examples of income are: sales income, rent/ subletting income, farming income, interest income, commission received, dividends received, discount received etc.
The accounting treatment of income is that:

a. Increase in income are credited (Cr).
b. Decrease are debited (Dr).

Each individual income should have its own distinct account separate account.
Example:
On 30th June 2022, received rent income by cash ksh.1200.
On 30th June 2022, sold goods by cash ksh.5000.
Transaction 1:

account affected :

rent income (Cr).
Cash paid (Dr).

Transaction 2:

account affected :

sales account (Cr).
Cash account (Dr).
